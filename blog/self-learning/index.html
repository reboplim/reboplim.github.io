Self-Learning In The Age Of Distraction

May 8, 2024

During 5th grade summer break, I visited my home country, Singapore, with my mom. She wanted to shop for clothes, so I decided to browse the local bookstore for something interesting to read. At the time, I just finished reading a book about Minecraft’s development process, and I was hungry to know more.

I remembered that Minecraft was created in Java, so I went to the section on programming languages and grabbed the first book on Java that caught my eye. I was immediately enthralled and overwhelmed; I spent the rest of the afternoon (and summer break!) writing Java programs.

Fast-forward 8 years, and I can confidently say that this decision has changed my life. Almost all of my programming knowledge is self-taught. I’m a pretty good programmer now, and I’m strengthening my foundations in mathematics and physics to pursue a career in research/“deep tech”. Some of my friends have asked me for advice on self-learning, so I’ll share some of my thoughts.

Self-Learning Is Messy

Study hard what interests you the most in the most undisciplined, irreverent and original manner possible.

Richard Feynman

Self-learning is a messy and spontaneous process. You’re going to read books while riding the bus, spend hours struggling to understand concepts, and run out of the shower to your desk just to test a new idea. The key to self-learning is to embrace that chaos.

Follow your intuition. Make sure the process is fun for yourself. I’ve seen many people follow a “syllabus” to learn programming, and they eventually give up because they spent too long learning theory without making fun projects with code.

Dive Deep

As an autodidact, you can dive deep into concepts without worrying about straying off a syllabus. Allow yourself to go down rabbit holes, the most common way to do so is to keep asking “why”.

Look at ideas from different perspectives. Find different ways to describe the same concept. Notice the links between different concepts and different fields. Play with your ideas to generate insights and intuition that is unique to you.

Practice Is A Necessity

For example, when the Riemann hypothesis first appeared in a paper in 1859, it was like magic: Here’s this amazing conjecture, out of nowhere. For 70 years, people talked about what a great thinker can do by pure thought alone. Then the mathematician Carl Siegel found Riemann’s scratch notes in the Göttingen archives. Riemann had actually done pages of calculations of zeros of the Riemann zeta function. Siegel’s famous words were, “So much for pure thought alone.”

Andrew Granville

Many people romanticize the processes of research and discovery. We often think that ideas and insights are created by strokes of creativity. This belief is also reinforced by mass media, because it’s more sensational to report what is discovered instead of the less glamorous process of how it was discovered.

Contrary to popular belief, researchers do a lot of manual and tedious work before gaining insights. In fact, the process of doing manual work gives them familiarity for the subject, just like an artist shaping clay with their bare hands. You should adopt the same approach with self-learning. Practice and hard work is necessary for an intuitive understanding of your subject.
